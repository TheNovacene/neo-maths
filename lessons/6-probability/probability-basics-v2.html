Focus Concept: Theoretical vs Experimental Probability

The theoretical probability of an event is what we expect to happen in a perfectly fair system. For example, the chance of rolling a 7 as the sum of two dice is 6 out of 36, or 1/6.

The experimental probability is what we actually observe when we do an experiment — like our horse race!

🎯 Use the grid in the app to complete the probability space diagram. Add together the two dice in each cell. How many ways are there to make each total?

💡 What patterns do you notice in the sums? Why is 7 the most common?

🤝 Connection — Whose story does this maths tell, and who does it connect?

Probability is used everywhere — from predicting the weather to designing fair games and testing medical treatments. But what happens when our expectations don’t match reality?

✊ Injustice can occur when probability is misunderstood — such as unfair algorithms or biased assumptions in policing and education.

🧵 Whose lives are affected when real-world outcomes don’t reflect theoretical models? How can we design systems that are fair, transparent, and evidence-based?

🎨 Creativity — How can you make, imagine, or express this in your own way?

🎨 Imagine each horse in this game has a personality. What kind of "person" is Horse 7? What about Horse 2?

✏ Draw a comic or write a story about the race. How do the horses feel about their chances?

🧠 In your own words, explain why some outcomes are more likely than others, even when everything is "fair."

🔄 Movement — How does this idea move — and how can you put it to work?

🎲 Use the interactive tool above to run the horse race. Roll the dice and watch the horses move based on the sum of the dice.

  • Before starting, make a prediction: Which horse will win?
  • As you play, record how many times each total appears.
  • Compare the experimental results to the theoretical probabilities.

📊 What do you notice over time? Does the actual data match what you expected?

👥 Buddy Challenge: Compare predictions and outcomes with a classmate. Whose prediction came closest to the final result? Why?

♿ Design Prompt: How would you redesign the game for someone who can’t use a mouse or who uses a screen reader?

🌙 Rest — Where’s the space to breathe, pace yourself, and let it settle?

🧘 How do you feel when your prediction is wrong? What happens in your body when you're surprised by the result?

🫁 Try this: breathe in for “Expect”, breathe out for “Observe”. Let go of outcomes and focus on noticing patterns.

🎯 Tap your fingers slowly from 2 to 12. Which numbers feel more “stable”? Which feel like long shots?

🌱 Nutrition · Final Task — What real-world need does this feed? · The Race Report

Submit the following:

  • 🧮 A completed probability space diagram showing all 36 dice combinations.
  • 📊 A table showing the number of times each total appeared during your race.
  • 📝 A short explanation of how the experimental results compared to the theoretical probabilities.
  • 💡 A design idea to make the game more inclusive or informative.

🎓 This task is assessed using the GCSE Maths Rubric:

  • Conceptual Understanding: Did you understand and explain the difference between theoretical and experimental probability?
  • Fluency & Accuracy: Did you complete the diagram and track the data correctly?
  • Application to Problems: Did you analyse outcomes and interpret patterns?
  • Independence & Reflection: Did you reflect on the unpredictability of outcomes and suggest improvements?
