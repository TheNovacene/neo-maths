Parallel and Perpendicular Lines
The secret handshake of gradients — Pure 1, Coordinate Geometry (Lesson 2)

Driving Question: Two lines never have to meet — or they can cross at a perfect right angle. How can a single number, the gradient, tell you which it will be before you draw anything?

In Lesson 1 you learned to write the equation of any straight line. This lesson is about the relationship between two lines — when they run forever side by side (parallel), and when they meet at a perfect right angle (perpendicular). The remarkable thing is that both relationships are hiding in the gradients alone. Open the panels in any order, and spend time in the explorer in The Designed World — rotating a line until it "clicks" into a right angle is where m₁m₂ = −1 stops being a rule to memorise and becomes something you can feel.

🧠 The Knowing World

Everything here rests on the gradient m from Lesson 1. Two lines, with gradients m₁ and m₂, relate in one of two special ways:

RelationshipCondition on gradientsIn words
Parallelm₁ = m₂Same steepness, so they never meet. (They are different lines only if their intercepts differ.)
Perpendicularm₁ · m₂ = −1They cross at a right angle. Equivalently, each gradient is the negative reciprocal of the other: m₂ = −1/m₁.

The "negative reciprocal" — the heart of the lesson

For perpendicular lines, the rule m₁m₂ = −1 can be reorganised into a recipe you'll use constantly: to get a gradient perpendicular to a given one, flip it and change its sign.

Negative-reciprocal examples:
• perpendicular to gradient 2 → −1/2
• perpendicular to gradient −3/4 → +4/3 (flip 3/4 to 4/3, change the sign)
• perpendicular to gradient −5 → +1/5
Notice the pattern: a gentle slope becomes a steep one, and an uphill line becomes a downhill one.

Putting it to work

Most exam questions combine this with Lesson 1's skill of writing a line through a point. The method is always the same three steps:

  1. Find the gradient of the given line (rearrange to y = mx + c if needed to read off m).
  2. Decide the new gradient: same for parallel, negative reciprocal for perpendicular.
  3. Use point–gradient form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) with the given point, then rearrange to the form the question wants.
Worked example (the spec's own). Find the line perpendicular to 3x + 4y = 18 passing through (2, 3).
• Gradient of the given line: rearrange to y = −¾x + 4.5, so its gradient is −3/4.
• Perpendicular gradient: negative reciprocal of −3/4 is +4/3.
• Through (2, 3): y − 3 = 4/3 (x − 2).
That last line is exactly the answer the specification gives for this example.
🌱 Seeds of Change (Pearson Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics 1, sections 2.1–2.2): Use the conditions for two straight lines to be parallel (m₁ = m₂) or perpendicular (m₁m₂ = −1); find the equation of a line parallel or perpendicular to a given line through a given point.

Two edge cases worth meeting: a horizontal line (gradient 0) is perpendicular to a vertical line (no gradient) — but you can't check this with m₁m₂ = −1, because a vertical line has no gradient to multiply. The product rule quietly assumes neither line is vertical. It's a good reminder that a formula always carries hidden conditions.

🌍 The Human Story

The right angle may be the single most important shape in human civilisation. Long before anyone wrote m₁m₂ = −1, people needed perpendicular lines to build — and they found ingenious ways to get them without any algebra at all.

  • The rope-stretchers of ancient Egypt. To re-mark field boundaries after the Nile's annual flood, surveyors called harpedonaptae ("rope-stretchers") used a loop of rope knotted into 12 equal sections. Pulled taut into a 3–4–5 triangle, it produces a guaranteed right angle — a physical version of Pythagoras's theorem, used in practice perhaps a thousand years before Pythagoras was born.
  • Master builders everywhere. The same need echoes across cultures: the Roman groma for laying out perpendicular streets, the mason's set square, the carpenter's try square. Getting a true right angle reliably is one of the oldest engineering problems there is.
  • Cities you can read. The grid plan — perpendicular streets crossing at right angles — appears independently in ancient Mohenjo-daro, in Roman colonial towns, and in modern Manhattan. A grid is, quite literally, a field of parallel and perpendicular lines, and it shapes how millions of people navigate every day.

There's a thinking point worth holding onto here. For most of history, "perpendicular" was a physical achievement — stretch this rope, square this corner. Descartes' coordinate idea (Lesson 1) let us capture it as a number relationship instead: two gradients whose product is −1. The same truth, moved from the hands into algebra. That move — turning a practical craft into a portable rule anyone can apply — is one of the quiet superpowers of mathematics.

🎨 The Expressive Self

Make the idea your own — pick whichever appeals. You only need to do one.

Explain the flip. In your own words (written, spoken, or recorded), explain why "flip and change sign" produces a perpendicular gradient. You've understood it when you can convince someone who only knows what a gradient is.
Perpendicular hunt. Photograph or sketch five real right angles around you — a window frame, a road junction, a tiled floor. For one of them, estimate the two gradients (relative to some "horizontal") and check whether your estimates multiply to roughly −1.
Design a grid. Sketch a small street map of an imaginary town using only parallel and perpendicular roads. Label two roads with equations that are genuinely parallel, and two that are genuinely perpendicular. Designing it correctly is the mathematics.
Make a memory hook. "Flip and change sign" is easy to half-remember and get wrong (people flip but forget the sign, or vice versa). Invent a phrase, image, or tiny story that locks both halves together for you.

Investigate with the explorer:
  1. Set the reference gradient to m₁ = 2. Drag the blue gradient until the verdict says "parallel". What is m₂? Now nudge it slightly — how sensitive is "parallel" to small changes?
  2. Keep m₁ = 2 and now hunt for "perpendicular". What value of m₂ does it click at? Check: does 2 × m₂ equal −1?
  3. Try m₁ = −3/4 (set it to −0.75) and use the "Snap to perpendicular" button. Read off m₂. Does it match the negative reciprocal +4/3 ≈ 1.33?
  4. Make the reference line nearly flat (m₁ close to 0). What happens to the perpendicular gradient m₂? Why does it run away to huge values, and what shape is the perpendicular line approaching?
  5. Generalise. Without the explorer, predict the perpendicular gradient for m₁ = 5, then for m₁ = −1/3. Use the buttons to check. Write the rule in your own words.

A deliberate wrinkle: push the reference gradient all the way to a very steep value and try to make the blue line perpendicular. As m₁ grows, the perpendicular m₂ shrinks toward 0 — and if you imagine m₁ becoming vertical (infinite), its perpendicular is perfectly horizontal (gradient 0). But notice the product rule can't express that case: a vertical line has no gradient to put in m₁m₂ = −1. The picture handles it; the formula politely declines. That gap is worth noticing.

💛 The Living Body

The perpendicular rule is a classic spot for a particular small frustration: you remember there's "a flip and a minus sign somewhere," but under pressure you can't recall whether you flip first, or where the sign goes, or whether it was −1 or +1. That fuzzy half-memory feeling is incredibly common and is not a sign you don't understand it — it's a sign the rule hasn't been anchored to a picture yet.

A few things that genuinely help here:

  • Anchor the rule to the explorer. If you've watched the lines click into a right angle as the product hits −1 a dozen times, your memory has something to hold onto that isn't just symbols. The picture is the backup your recall reaches for.
  • Sense-check with steepness. Perpendicular lines always trade "gentle for steep" and "uphill for downhill." If you compute a perpendicular gradient and it doesn't flip the steepness and the direction, you've slipped — a quick, calming self-check that needs no formula.
  • Two small steps, not one big rule. "Flip" then "change the sign" — naming them as two separate moves makes each one easy to check, instead of trying to do both at once and dropping half.

Two reflections to sit with:

  • When you think "perpendicular," do you picture the right angle, or reach for the formula first? Either is fine — knowing your instinct tells you which to lean on.
  • Was there a moment in the explorer where the rule suddenly made sense? What caused it? That's worth remembering about how you learn.
🛠 Final Task

Build a "Parallel & Perpendicular Toolkit." Create a one- or two-page guide (handwritten and photographed, typed, or a slide) that teaches both relationships to a learner who has just finished Lesson 1. It must contain:

  1. The two conditions clearly stated (m₁ = m₂ and m₁m₂ = −1), each with a one-line plain-English explanation.
  2. The negative-reciprocal recipe ("flip and change sign"), with three of your own examples — including at least one negative gradient and one fractional gradient.
  3. A worked "parallel" problem: find the line parallel to a line of your choosing, through a point of your choosing, giving the answer in the form ax + by + c = 0. Show every step.
  4. A worked "perpendicular" problem: the same, but perpendicular, and giving the answer in y = mx + c form. Show every step.
  5. The vertical/horizontal edge case — explain in a sentence or two why a horizontal and a vertical line are perpendicular even though the product rule m₁m₂ = −1 can't be used to prove it.
  6. One real-world connection — a sentence linking right angles or parallels to something outside maths (building, navigation, design, or your own example from The Human Story).
  7. A short reflection (2–3 sentences): the part of the negative-reciprocal rule you find easiest to slip on, and how you'll catch it.
