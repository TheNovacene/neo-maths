Graphs and Transformations
Same shape, new place — Pure 1, Algebra and Functions (Lesson 6)

Driving Question: If I already know the shape of one curve, how much can I learn about a whole family of related curves without plotting a single extra point?

Welcome. This lesson is about a quietly powerful idea: most of the curves you will ever meet are not new shapes at all — they are familiar shapes that have been moved, stretched, or flipped. Once you can read those four moves, you can sketch hundreds of graphs from a handful you already know. Take your time, open the panels in any order that suits you, and play with the explorer in Movement until the patterns feel like yours.

🔎 Reflection — What patterns can you notice — and what do you notice about your own thinking?

Start with any function and call it f(x). Its graph is y = f(x) — a fixed shape sitting in the plane. There are exactly four simple transformations you need at this level, and each changes the graph in one specific way.

The four transformations

New equationWhat it does to the graphDirection of the effect
y = f(x) + aTranslation up/down by aVertical — behaves as you'd expect (add a → moves up by a)
y = f(x + a)Translation left/right by aHorizontal — the "wrong" way: f(x + a) moves left by a
y = a·f(x)Vertical stretch, scale factor aStretches away from the x-axis; if a is negative, also reflects in the x-axis
y = f(ax)Horizontal stretch, scale factor 1/aAgain the "wrong" way: bigger a → squashes the graph toward the y-axis

The single most important pattern to notice — the one that separates students who memorise from students who understand:

The "inside vs outside" rule. Changes outside the function (f(x) + a, a·f(x)) affect the graph vertically and behave intuitively. Changes inside the brackets (f(x + a), f(ax)) affect the graph horizontally and behave counter-intuitively — the opposite of what the sign or size seems to suggest. If you only remember one thing, remember this.

Why does "inside" go the wrong way? Think about y = f(x + 3). To get the same output the original graph had at x = 0, you now need x + 3 = 0, i.e. x = −3. The feature that was at 0 has moved to −3 — three to the left. The bracket "gets there first," so it pulls the graph in the opposite direction. That one sentence explains both horizontal cases.

🌱 Seed of Change (Pearson Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics 1, section 1.12): Understand and apply the effect of the transformations y = af(x), y = f(x) + a, y = f(x + a) and y = f(ax) on the graph of y = f(x). Students should be able to apply one of these transformations to any of the standard functions (quadratics, cubics, reciprocals, sine, cosine, tangent) and, given the graph of any y = f(x), sketch the result.

A note on what we know before this lesson (section 1.11): you are expected to already recognise the basic shapes — simple cubics, and the reciprocal curves y = k/x and y = k/x² (with their asymptotes). Transformations take those known shapes as the starting clay.

🤝 Connection — Whose story does this maths tell, and who does it connect?

The idea that you can describe a complicated picture as a simple picture plus a set of moves is one of the most reused ideas in all of human culture — far beyond mathematics.

  • Music. A melody transposed into a different key is the same tune shifted — a horizontal "translation" in pitch. Play it twice as slow and you have a stretch. Composers have built whole forms (canon, fugue) out of translating and reflecting a single line.
  • Animation and games. Every character on a screen is a base shape that gets translated, scaled, and reflected dozens of times a second. The maths in this lesson is, almost exactly, the maths inside a graphics engine — a 2D sprite walking left is f(x + a) in action.
  • Pattern and ornament. The tilework of the Alhambra in Granada, and patterns in textiles and pottery across many cultures, are generated by repeating one motif under translations and reflections. The mathematicians who later formalised this called the complete set of such moves a symmetry group.

There's a fairness angle worth pausing on, too. Transformation thinking is a great leveller: it means you do not need to memorise the graph of every function separately. If you genuinely understand four moves applied to a few base shapes, you can reconstruct an enormous amount from very little. Mathematics rewards understanding structure over hoarding facts — and that's good news if memorising long lists has never been your strength.

🎨 Creativity — How can you make, imagine, or express this in your own way?

This is where you make the idea yours. Pick whichever of these appeals — you only need to do one.

Explain it to someone. In your own words (spoken aloud, written, or recorded as a short voice note), explain why f(x + a) moves the graph left when a is positive. You've understood it when you can convince someone who's confused — no formula, just the reasoning.
Find a metaphor. The "inside the bracket goes the wrong way" rule trips everyone up at first. Invent a metaphor or memory hook that makes it stick for you. (One student pictured the bracket as an impatient friend who always arrives early, so everything has to happen sooner — i.e. further left. Yours can be sillier.)
Sketch a family. Take one base shape you like — a parabola, a cubic, or a sine wave — and hand-draw four members of its family: the original, one translated, one stretched, one reflected. Label each with its equation. Make it beautiful if you want; the labelling is the mathematics.
Tell its story. Write a few sentences narrating a single point's journey as the graph is transformed: "I started at (2, 4). When the curve was shifted up 3, I rose to (2, 7). When it was reflected in the x-axis, I…" Watching one point is often clearer than watching the whole curve.

Investigate with the explorer:
  1. Pick f(x) = x² and the transformation f(x) + a. Where does the lowest point (the vertex) go as you increase a? Now switch to f(x + a). Which way does the vertex move, and does that surprise you?
  2. Still on x², try a·f(x) with a = 2, then a = 0.5, then a = −1. Describe in words what each does. What is special about a = −1?
  3. Choose f(x) = 1/x. This curve has two asymptotes (the axes). Apply f(x) + a and watch the horizontal asymptote. Then apply f(x + a) and watch the vertical asymptote. Can you predict where each asymptote lands before you let go of the slider?
  4. Choose sin(x) and the transformation f(ax). As a grows from 1 to 3, how many waves now fit in the same width? Write a rule connecting a to the number of waves.
  5. Generalise. For the point that starts at (0, f(0)), write down — in terms of a — where it ends up under each of the four transformations. (This is the heart of the whole topic in four short lines.)

A deliberate wrinkle to notice: for the reciprocal 1/x and for tan-like curves, transformations move the asymptotes too, not just the visible curve. The explorer plots these by "lifting the pen" across the break, so you'll see the curve correctly split into branches — a reminder that a transformation acts on the entire function, including the parts that run off to infinity.

🌙 Rest — Where’s the space to breathe, pace yourself, and let it settle?

This topic has a particular emotional texture worth naming. The vertical moves feel easy and obvious. Then the horizontal moves go "the wrong way," and a lot of people feel a small jolt of "wait, that's backwards — am I getting this wrong?" That feeling is not a sign you're struggling. It's a sign you've reached exactly the point the lesson is designed around. Everyone meets that jolt.

A few things that help:

  • Watch one point, not the whole curve. When the whole graph moving feels overwhelming, pick a single feature — a vertex, an intercept, a peak — and track just that. The brain handles one moving dot far more calmly than a whole sliding shape.
  • Say the counter-intuitive rule out loud. "Inside the bracket, the opposite of what it looks like." Naming the trap shrinks it.
  • Let the slider do the convincing. You don't have to believe the rule on faith. Drag the slider, watch it happen ten times, and let your eyes teach your intuition. That's a completely legitimate way to learn.

Two quick reflections to sit with for a moment:

  • Which of the four transformations felt most natural to you, and which felt most slippery? There's no right answer — noticing it is the point.
  • When the explorer showed you something you didn't expect, what did you do? Pause and look, or push on? Either is fine — but knowing your own habit is useful information about how you learn.
🌱 Nutrition · Final Task — What real-world need does this feed?

Build a "Transformation Field Guide." Create a one- or two-page guide (handwritten and photographed, typed, a slide, or a short screen-recording — your choice of form) that would teach the four transformations to a learner who has never met them. Your guide must contain:

  1. A base shape of your choosing (parabola, cubic, reciprocal, or sine), drawn clearly with its equation.
  2. All four transformations applied to it, each on its own small sketch, each labelled with both the new equation and a one-line description of the move. Use a specific value of a for each (not just the general form).
  3. A clear explanation of the "inside vs outside" rule — in your own words — including why the horizontal moves go the counter-intuitive way. A worked single-point argument (tracking one coordinate) earns full marks here.
  4. One "combination" example that goes slightly beyond the single-transformation requirement: take your base shape, apply a vertical shift and a reflection (e.g. y = −f(x) + 2), and sketch the result. Explain the order you applied the moves and why.
  5. One real-world connection — a sentence or two linking a transformation to something outside maths (music, animation, ornament, or your own example from Connection).
  6. A short reflection (2–3 sentences): which transformation you found hardest, and what finally made it click.
