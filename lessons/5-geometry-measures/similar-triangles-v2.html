Similar Triangles

🔎 Reflection — What patterns can you notice — and what do you notice about your own thinking?
Mathematicians define two triangles as similar if they have the same shape but not necessarily the same size. This means:
  • All corresponding angles are equal
  • All corresponding sides are in the same ratio
In this activity, you'll explore these properties by performing a dilation transformation from a fixed point and observing the resulting triangle.
🤝 Connection — Whose story does this maths tell, and who does it connect?
Architects, engineers, and artists have long relied on similar shapes to scale designs accurately. Understanding similarity helps us build bridges, design models, and replicate artworks with precision, regardless of scale.
🎨 Creativity — How can you make, imagine, or express this in your own way?
Can you use your intuition to predict how the shape of the triangle changes when you scale it up or down? What visual cues help you notice that the shape remains the same while the size changes?
🔄 Movement — How does this idea move — and how can you put it to work?
Use the interactive dilation tool above to:
  • Drag the vertex A and observe how the triangle scales from that point
  • Adjust the scale factor using the slider
  • Compare the side lengths and angles between the original and the scaled triangle
  • Record and analyze the ratios of corresponding sides
Prompts to consider:
  • What remains unchanged after dilation?
  • Do the angles change?
  • Are the side ratios always equal?
🌙 Rest — Where’s the space to breathe, pace yourself, and let it settle?
As you move points and adjust the scale factor, notice how your physical interactions help you understand abstract ideas like proportionality and geometric transformation. Your spatial awareness becomes part of your mathematical thinking.
🌱 Nutrition · Final Task — What real-world need does this feed?
Write a short explanation or create a diagram that shows how you know the two triangles are similar based on your observations. Can you state a rule for triangle similarity based on your findings?
