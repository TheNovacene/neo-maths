Solving Quadratic Equations by Factorisation

GCSE Maths | Pearson Edexcel (Higher) | 40–60 mins | Inquiry-Based | Neurodivergent‑Friendly

🔎 Reflection — What patterns can you notice — and what do you notice about your own thinking?

We begin by asking: given a quadratic like \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0\), how do we find the values of \(x\)? Can factorisation help us “undo” the equation?

Exploration prompts

  • Start with simple quadratics (monic): \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0\), \(x^2 + 7x + 10 = 0\).
  • Factor the left side: \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = (x + 2)(x + 3)\).
  • What does setting \((x + 2)(x + 3) = 0\) tell you about \(x\)? (Zero product rule → one factor must be zero.)
  • Generalise: if \((x + m)(x + n) = 0\), then \(x = -m\) or \(x = -n\).

Formal steps

  1. Write the quadratic in standard form: \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\).
  2. Factor the quadratic into two bracketed expressions.
  3. Apply the zero product rule: set each factor = 0.
  4. Solve each resulting linear equation for \(x\).

Quick check

  • Solve \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0\).
  • Solve \(x^2 - 3x + 2 = 0\).
  • Does \(x^2 + 4x + 5 = 0\) have real integer solutions? Why or why not?
🤝 Connection — Whose story does this maths tell, and who does it connect?

Factorisation of equations has roots (no pun intended) in the history of algebra. For centuries, mathematicians solved geometric problems by translating them into equations. The technique of breaking down expressions to find unknown solutions empowered leaps in science, engineering, and economics.

🎨 Creativity — How can you make, imagine, or express this in your own way?
  • Illustrate the zero‑product rule as “if a × b = 0, then a = 0 or b = 0” using visuals or metaphors (like a broken chain link).
  • Write a short dialogue between two learners solving a quadratic, showing their reasoning and pitfalls.
  • Record yourself teaching someone how to factor and solve a quadratic by factorisation.

🌙 Rest — Where’s the space to breathe, pace yourself, and let it settle?

Use your arms to act out “x + 2” and “x + 3” — intersecting or crossing paths to show they multiply to 0. Then open your hands as you reveal the solutions!

🌱 Nutrition · Final Task — What real-world need does this feed?

Task: Solve at least 5 monic quadratics by factorising. Some should have negative solutions, some no integer roots. Reflect on patterns and challenges.  Then try at least 5 non-monic quadratics.

  • Conceptual Understanding — Show the link between factorisation and solving for roots.
  • Fluency & Accuracy — Solve and explain with correct reasoning.
  • Application — Describe how factorisation might help in real-world modelling (e.g. area problems).
  • Independence & Reflection — What makes a quadratic “nice” to factor? What’s your strategy when it’s not?
