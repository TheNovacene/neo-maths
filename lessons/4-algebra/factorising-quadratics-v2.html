Factorising Monic Quadratics (Inquiry Approach)

GCSE Maths | Pearson Edexcel (Higher) | 40–60 mins | Neurodivergent‑Friendly

🔎 Reflection — What patterns can you notice — and what do you notice about your own thinking?

We begin with a question: when can a quadratic of the form \(x^2 + bx + c\) be written as \((x + m)(x + n)\)? What must \(m\) and \(n\) do?

Exploration prompts

  • Choose some quadratics: e.g. \(x^2 + 5x + 6\), \(x^2 + 7x + 10\), \(x^2 + 4x + 5\).
  • List all integer pairs \((m, n)\) whose product = \(c\). Which of those also have sum = \(b\)?
  • Notice: if \(m + n = b\) and \(m \cdot n = c\), then \(x^2 + bx + c = (x + m)(x + n)\).
  • Observe: Some quadratics (e.g. \(x^2 + 4x + 5\)) have no integer factor pairs that sum to 4 — those are *not factorable over the integers*.

Formal statement

If \(x^2 + bx + c\) factorises with integer roots, then there exist integers \(m, n\) such that:

  • \(m + n = b\)
  • \(m \cdot n = c\)
  • Hence \(x^2 + bx + c = (x + m)(x + n)\).

Quick check

  • Factorise \(x^2 + 8x + 15\).
  • Does \(x^2 + 6x + 14\) factorise with integer roots? Why / why not?
  • Factorise \(x^2 - 3x + 2\).
🤝 Connection — Whose story does this maths tell, and who does it connect?

Historically, algebraic factorisation has been tied to solving polynomial equations and understanding roots. Mathematicians in the Middle Ages and the Renaissance used methods of factoring to find unknowns and design curves. Even in today’s cryptography and signal processing, factoring polynomials is foundational.

🎨 Creativity — How can you make, imagine, or express this in your own way?
  • Create a “factor map”: pick a quadratic and draw all integer factor pairs of \(c\), then mark which pair sums to \(b\).
  • Write a mini dialogue: “Student A: It doesn’t factor. Student B: Let’s test m, n pairs…”
  • Record a short voice or video explanation of your reasoning for one quadratic.

🌙 Rest — Where’s the space to breathe, pace yourself, and let it settle?

Stand and balance: for each integer pair \((m,n)\) that you test, lift one foot for \(m\) steps and the other foot for \(n\) steps. Reflect on how combining them gives \(b\).

🌱 Nutrition · Final Task — What real-world need does this feed?

Task: Create a set of 6 monic quadratics (some factorable, some not) and challenge a peer to find the factor pair for each.

  • Conceptual Understanding — Explain why \(m + n = b\) and \(m \cdot n = c\) is necessary.
  • Fluency & Accuracy — Factor correctly or show no integer factorisation.
  • Application — Use quadratics that model area problems or equations.
  • Independence & Reflection — Reflect: Which quadratics were hardest and why?
