Solving Quadratic Equations by Completing the Square

GCSE Maths | Pearson Edexcel (Higher) | 45–65 mins | Inquiry-Based | Neurodivergent-Friendly

🔎 Reflection — What patterns can you notice — and what do you notice about your own thinking?

What if we could turn any quadratic into a perfect square? Completing the square helps us do just that!

Key ideas

  • Completing the square rewrites a quadratic in the form: \( ax^2 + bx + c \Rightarrow a(x + p)^2 + q \).
  • This method works even when factorising doesn't — especially for non-monic or irrational roots.
  • It’s also a gateway to understanding the **vertex form** of a parabola.

Quick Check

  • Can you complete the square for \(x^2 + 6x + 5\)?
  • What would you add and subtract to complete the square for \(x^2 + 8x\)?
🤝 Connection — Whose story does this maths tell, and who does it connect?

Completing the square dates back to ancient Babylon and was later formalised by Persian mathematicians like Al-Khwarizmi. It laid the groundwork for solving all kinds of quadratic equations — with or without modern tools.

🎨 Creativity — How can you make, imagine, or express this in your own way?
  • Use a square diagram to explain why the method works visually.
  • Write a dialogue between two students solving \(x^2 + 10x + c\) in different ways.
  • Record a short explanation teaching the steps to someone younger.

🌙 Rest — Where’s the space to breathe, pace yourself, and let it settle?

Form a human square with friends — four people for the sides. Now “complete” the square by adding a small square piece to balance the edges. Move around and explain the steps as you go!

🌱 Nutrition · Final Task — What real-world need does this feed?

Task: Solve three quadratic equations by completing the square. One should result in irrational roots.

  • Understanding — Show the full process of completing the square.
  • Reasoning — Explain why you added and subtracted certain values.
  • Application — Choose one real-world scenario where this might apply.
