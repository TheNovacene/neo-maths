Solving Quadratic Equations Using the Quadratic Formula

GCSE Maths | Inquiry-Based | 40–60 mins | Neurodivergent-Friendly

🔎 Reflection — What patterns can you notice — and what do you notice about your own thinking?

The quadratic formula provides a universal method for solving any quadratic equation of the form \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \).

Key Ideas

  • The quadratic formula:
    \[ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \]
  • The expression under the square root is called the discriminant: \( D = b^2 - 4ac \)
  • It determines the nature of the roots:
    • If \( D > 0 \): two real roots
    • If \( D = 0 \): one real root
    • If \( D < 0 \): two complex roots

Try it

  • Solve \( x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0 \) using the formula.
  • Solve \( 2x^2 + 3x - 2 = 0 \) and interpret the discriminant.
  • Try a quadratic that has no real roots, such as \( x^2 + x + 1 = 0 \).
🤝 Connection — Whose story does this maths tell, and who does it connect?

The quadratic formula dates back to ancient Babylonian and Greek mathematics. Mathematicians sought patterns in area and length, leading them to develop systematic methods like this one. Can you think of why a universal method for all quadratics might have been so important in the past?

Task

  • Try different values for \( a \), \( b \), and \( c \) and observe the roots.
  • What happens as \( b^2 - 4ac \) changes sign?
  • Can you make the discriminant exactly zero?
🌙 Rest — Where’s the space to breathe, pace yourself, and let it settle?

Form a "human equation": groups of three learners represent \( a \), \( b \), and \( c \). Rearrange and act out the steps of the quadratic formula to discover the roots. Use coloured cards or strings to visualise the operations.

  • Application — Create a real-world problem that results in a quadratic equation.
