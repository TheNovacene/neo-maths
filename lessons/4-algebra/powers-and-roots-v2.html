Powers and Roots

GCSE Maths | Pearson Edexcel (Higher) | 40–60 mins | Neurodivergent-Friendly

🔎 Reflection — What patterns can you notice — and what do you notice about your own thinking?

Powers tell us how many times a number is multiplied by itself. Roots reverse this process. How do powers and roots relate to one another?

Key ideas

  • Base and exponent: In \(3^4\), 3 is the base, and 4 is the power (exponent).
  • Multiplying powers: \(a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}\)
  • Dividing powers: \(a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n}\)
  • Power of a power: \((a^m)^n = a^{m \times n}\)
  • Roots: \(\sqrt{a} = a^{1/2}\), \(\sqrt[3]{a} = a^{1/3}\)
  • Negative powers: \(a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}\)

Try these

  • Evaluate \(2^3\), \(5^2\), and \(10^0\).
  • Simplify \(x^4 \times x^3\) and \(x^5 \div x^2\).
  • Find \(\sqrt{81}\), \(\sqrt[3]{27}\), and \(9^{-2}\).
🤝 Connection — Whose story does this maths tell, and who does it connect?

From ancient Babylonian squares to modern exponential growth, powers and roots have shaped science, architecture, and data. What might we have misunderstood without them?

Challenge

  1. Match expressions like \(2^3\), \(3^{-2}\), and \(\sqrt[3]{64}\) to their values.
  2. Estimate powers and roots for non-perfect cases.
  3. Compare base changes and observe exponential patterns.

Use your arms to show exponential growth—start small and double each time! Then mimic square and cube roots by shrinking back to 1. Use body language to represent abstract change.
